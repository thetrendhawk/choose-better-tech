## Effectiveness, Limitations, and Independent Evidence

**What appears well-supported.** The best-supported parts of IncogniŌĆÖs effectiveness story are procedural rather than outcome-percentage claims. DeloitteŌĆÖs limited-assurance report verified the existence of recurring request workflows, a covered-broker base of **420+**, receipt of removal confirmations from **420+** brokers, and processing at scale. The report also describes test work on samples of private and public brokers and reconciliation of reported metrics to system logs and dashboards. That is solid evidence that Incogni is actually sending and tracking requests at scale. It is **not** equivalent to a guarantee that every user will be removed everywhere or that every removal stays permanent. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn17view0Ņłéturn18view1Ņłü

**How Incogni verifies and follows up.** Incogni says a request can show statuses such as **Completed, In Progress, Failed,** and **Escalated** in the dashboard, and it says failed submissions are automatically re-submitted. Its broker pages also expose a broker-specific request history and the **next request due**. Separately, Incogni says it uses an algorithm to predict whether private-data brokers likely have your information because many such brokers lack public-facing search tools. That means some requests may be sent to brokers that do not actually hold your data. For public people-search sites, Incogni says it performs checks and can file complaints when a broker appears not to have complied. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn8view0Ņłéturn8view2Ņłéturn36search5Ņłü

**Recurring removals and reappearance risk.** IncogniŌĆÖs strongest design choice for mainstream users is that it treats removal as an **ongoing maintenance process** rather than a one-time job. ## Privacy, Security, and Trustworthiness

**What data Incogni needs from users.** Incogni cannot work without collecting enough identifying information to match the user against broker records and to act as the userŌĆÖs authorized agent. Its public materials say users sign an authorization form so Incogni can contact brokers on their behalf, and help-center documentation says users can store up to **three email addresses**, **three home addresses**, and ŌĆö for U.S. users ŌĆö up to **three phone numbers**. **Privacy model assessment.** IncogniŌĆÖs privacy policy says it needs personal data to provide the service, that some of that data is shared with data brokers solely to exercise your deletion / opt-out rights, that data is retained as long as needed to provide services and fulfill legal obligations, and that Incogni uses third-party processors listed in a subprocessor list. Its public materials say data is processed on **EU-based infrastructure**, with **encrypted data transfers** and optional **2FA**. The strongest evidence on ŌĆ£does Incogni sell my data?ŌĆØ is mixed but reasonably favorable: this remains fundamentally a **vendor claim** in its privacy policy and marketing, but DeloitteŌĆÖs limited-assurance engagement also concluded that nothing came to its attention indicating Incogni sold customer data during 2024. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn5view0Ņłéturn6view0Ņłéturn13view0Ņłéturn17view0Ņłü

**Risks created by using any removal service.** The category-level risk is unavoidable: to remove your data, the vendor needs enough identifying information to locate you and to submit that information back to brokers. Incogni mitigates some of that risk by leaning on legal-authorized-agent workflows and suppression files, and its help center states that brokers are not permitted to reuse request-submission data beyond fulfilling the request, though they may keep the minimum necessary in a **suppression file**. That is directionally reassuring, but it is still a trust-based model. A user who is especially sensitive about concentrating personal data with any third party should view this as a meaningful tradeoff, not a zero-risk transaction. **Independent audits, assurance, and verification.** This is where Incogni stands out. The Deloitte report is a real differentiator because it goes beyond generic marketing and spells out testing scope, limits, and conclusions. It also explicitly states what was **out of scope**, including broader IT-security testing, encryption testing, and generalized legal-compliance evaluation. That means the report should be read as **process assurance on removal operations and non-sale of data**, not as a full security audit or a warranty of legal compliance. **SOC 2 and broader security claims.** IncogniŌĆÖs ŌĆ£About usŌĆØ page says it is **SOC 2 Type II audited** and mentions encrypted transfers and optional 2FA. **Trustworthiness judgment.** On balance, IncogniŌĆÖs privacy and security model looks **more trustworthy than average for this category**, chiefly because: it sits within a known Surfshark / Nord Security corporate ecosystem; it publishes a public broker list; it exposes broker-level dashboard data; it has a real third-party assurance report on core operating claims; and it does not rely solely on anonymous marketing language.
