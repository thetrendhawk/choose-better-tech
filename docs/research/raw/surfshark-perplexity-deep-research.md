## Privacy, security, audits, and open-source status

### Privacy policy and data handling

**Major claim:** Surfshark states that it does **not collect browsing history, visited IPs, session information, used bandwidth, or network traffic**. **Classification:** **Vendor Claim.** That is the current language of the May 21, 2026 privacy policy. citeturn7view2

**Major claim:** Surfshark’s privacy posture is **not literally “zero data collected.”** The company says its servers temporarily keep **user ID and/or IP address and connection-to-server timestamps**, and that this data is deleted within **15 minutes after the session ends**. **Classification:** **Verified Fact.** This is stated directly in the current privacy policy. For a buyer dossier, this matters because it narrows the practical meaning of “no logs” to “no activity logs,” not “no operational metadata whatsoever.” citeturn7view2

**Major claim:** Surfshark retains ordinary account and billing data, including email address, subscription details, and some payment-related data, for longer periods than VPN-session metadata. **Classification:** **Verified Fact.** The current privacy policy says account data is held while the subscription is active and for two years after the last login, and that payment-related information may be retained for ten years for tax/accounting purposes. citeturn7view2turn7view0

**Major claim:** Surfshark’s current transparency reporting shows that its logging model can materially limit what it can disclose to authorities. **Classification:** **Verified Fact.** In the current transparency report, Surfshark says that in April 2026 it complied with a legally binding Amsterdam court warrant, but because of its no-logs approach the information available to disclose was limited to confirmation of account existence and payment-related information. citeturn13view0

**Major claim:** There is a **material open question** around antivirus-related data minimization. **Classification:** **Open Question.** TechRadar reported in March 2026 that Surfshark had stopped collecting identifiable malware-related data and would fully anonymize malware statistics, but the current May 2026 privacy policy still describes processing malware name and type, country, OS, and user ID before anonymization after one year. Those two records do not line up cleanly, so a repository-ready publication should request clarification from Surfshark before finalizing privacy language. citeturn26view0turn6view2

### Security features

**Major claim:** Surfshark offers a mainstream security feature set that is unusually broad for a beginner-oriented VPN. **Classification:** **Verified Fact** for feature availability; **Independent Finding** for the “unusually broad” judgment. Official feature pages list kill switch, split tunneling, obfuscation, MultiHop, NoBorders, CleanWeb, rotating IP, static IP, RAM-only servers, DNS leak protection, and 2FA support; reviewers consistently cite this breadth as one of Surfshark’s selling points. citeturn29view3turn29view1turn29view2turn37search0turn37search1turn32view0turn19view0

**Major claim:** Surfshark currently supports **Dausos, WireGuard, OpenVPN, and IKEv2**, with post-quantum protection promoted for Dausos and WireGuard in the apps. **Classification:** **Vendor Claim** for the protocol and post-quantum statements; **Verified Fact** that Surfshark is publicly making those claims in its current protocol documentation. The same page says the Windows app no longer supports IKEv2 directly in-app, though it can still be configured manually via Windows settings. citeturn28view1

**Major claim:** Surfshark’s network is marketed as **100% RAM-only**. **Classification:** **Vendor Claim.** Surfshark’s trust center and server pages both state that the network is RAM-only and that data is wiped on reboot. citeturn13view1turn28view0

**Major claim:** Surfshark’s Nexus stack is a distinctive product differentiator, but it is also a **Surfshark-specific architectural claim rather than an industry-standard primitive with broad external validation**. **Classification:** **Vendor Claim.** Surfshark says Nexus uses software-defined networking to connect its servers into a single network and powers rotating IP and dynamic MultiHop. That may be meaningful, but the strongest evidence in the record is Surfshark’s own description plus the 2026 Cure53 assessment specific to Dausos/architecture. citeturn28view2turn36view0

### Independent audits

**Major claim:** Surfshark has more third-party audit activity than many budget-positioned VPN brands, and that is a real positive. **Classification:** **Independent Finding.** The trust center and audit summaries document at least the following relevant milestones: Cure53 browser-extension review in 2018; Cure53 infrastructure/security assessment in 2021; Deloitte no-logs assurance in 2023; Deloitte no-logs assurance again in 2025; a SecuRing 2025 application security assessment; and a 2026 Cure53 security assessment of the Dausos architecture and cryptography. citeturn13view1turn36view1turn15view1turn16view0turn15view0turn36view0

**Major claim:** The strongest audit-backed privacy signal in this dossier is that **Deloitte independently reviewed Surfshark’s no-logs controls in both 2023 and 2025**. **Classification:** **Independent Finding.** Surfshark’s disclosures say Deloitte reviewed server configuration, deployment, privacy-related settings, and internal processes in assessing compliance with the no-logs policy. The detailed reports are not fully public, which limits outside scrutiny, but repeated Big Four assurance work is still materially better than an unaudited no-logs claim. citeturn15view1turn16view0

**Major claim:** The 2025 SecuRing application review produced **no critical vulnerabilities** across the tested web, desktop, mobile, and browser-extension scope. **Classification:** **Independent Finding.** The signed SecuRing assessment confirmation says the production-environment penetration test covered Surfshark desktop apps, mobile apps, browser extensions, and web applications and found no critical vulnerabilities, while providing further recommendations. citeturn15view0turn35view0turn35view1

**Major claim:** The 2026 Cure53 Dausos audit is a meaningful but **narrower protocol/architecture audit**, not a blanket certification of the entire Surfshark service. **Classification:** **Independent Finding.** Cure53 states that the scope focused on Surfshark VPN Dausos architecture and cryptography, found no Critical or High-severity findings within the actual Dausos protocol itself, and recommended a follow-up assessment plus formal protocol specification and threat modeling. ### Performance

**Major claim:** Surfshark is a **fast VPN in pure throughput terms**. **Classification:** **Independent Finding.** TechRadar’s 2026 testing reported median WireGuard download speeds of **1021 Mbps** to the nearest server, **935 Mbps** on Europe-to-US long-distance tests, and **825 Mbps** on OpenVPN, while Tom’s Guide also describes Surfshark as one of the fastest services it has tested. citeturn20view1turn10search4turn22search12

**Major claim:** Raw throughput is not the whole story: latency and jitter appear less impressive than the headline speed numbers suggest. **Classification:** **Independent Finding.** TechRadar says Surfshark showed considerably higher latency and jitter than other top VPNs in its latest tests, which can translate into more lag for gaming and more buffering in marginal streaming situations. Security.org’s testing is more favorable on some devices, which suggests the real-world answer depends on platform and route. citeturn20view1turn22search6

### Streaming

**Major claim:** Surfshark is **capable for streaming**, but the evidence set does **not support claiming consistently best-in-class reliability across services and regions**. **Classification:** **Independent Finding.** Tom’s Guide says Surfshark performed a “clean sweep” in its latest streaming tests and calls it a top streaming pick, while TechRadar found it inconsistent on several major targets, including trouble with some Netflix regions, Disney+, and logged-out US YouTube tests. This means streaming support should be treated as **volatile** and verified close to publication. citeturn26view2turn20view2turn20view0

**Major claim:** For mainstream users, the practical takeaway is that Surfshark is usually a **good streaming VPN**, but if streaming reliability is the single most important purchase criterion, stronger alternatives may exist. **Classification:** **Industry Consensus.** That is the direction of TechRadar’s recommendation toward NordVPN for streaming-focused buyers and the more mixed cross-publication evidence overall. citeturn26view3turn20view2turn19view2

### Torrenting and gaming

**Major claim:** Surfshark is a **good casual torrenting choice** but not an ideal **advanced torrenting** choice. **Classification:** **Industry Consensus.** Tom’s Guide says Surfshark is very capable for torrenting and supports full P2P traffic on every server but lacks port forwarding; TechRadar ranks it among top torrenting VPNs for value and speed but says it lacks torrent-specific features; Surfshark’s own materials state that port forwarding is not supported for security reasons. citeturn25news30turn23news26turn25search4

**Major claim:** Surfshark is best understood as a **budget gaming VPN for casual players**, not the strongest low-latency pick for competitive users. **Classification:** **Independent Finding.** Security.org’s gaming testing found Surfshark delivered the best Windows latency in its set at **72 ms**, but TechRadar’s core Surfshark review said latency and jitter were materially worse than other top VPNs in its latest lab work. ## Independent reviews, common criticisms, frequently asked questions, and open questions

### Independent-review consensus

Across the strongest independent sources in this research set, there is broad agreement that Surfshark is one of the market’s best **budget-to-mainstream** VPNs. WIRED positions it as the **best VPN for beginners**; Tom’s Guide repeatedly highlights its low price, beginner-friendly design, and strong broader value; Security.org gives it a high score while calling out temporary IP retention and Netherlands jurisdiction as caveats; and TechRadar ranks it as a top overall and budget choice while being noticeably harsher on streaming consistency and latency. That combination produces a stable canonical takeaway: Surfshark is easy to recommend for ordinary users, but the recommendation should be framed with caveats instead of as an unqualified “best for everyone.” **Classification:** **Industry Consensus.** citeturn32view0turn26view2turn19view0turn19view1

### Common criticisms

The most persistent criticism is **limited transparency relative to open-source rivals**, especially Proton. The second is **promotional pricing versus renewal pricing**, with multiple reviewers warning that the cheap entry price can become much less attractive if users forget to disable auto-renewal. The third is **feature tradeoffs for power users**, especially the lack of port forwarding and the lighter set of deep technical controls. The fourth is **mixed streaming consistency in some tests**. The fifth is privacy nuance: Surfshark’s policy is far stronger than many mass-market alternatives, but it still describes temporary session metadata and extensive ordinary account/billing retention, so reviewers and repository editors should avoid oversimplified “zero logs, period” wording. **Classification:** **Industry Consensus** for the review-based criticisms; **Verified Fact** for the official renewal and policy details. citeturn32view0turn26view2turn26view1turn20view2turn25search4turn34view0turn7view2turn7view0

### Frequently asked questions

**Is Surfshark a true no-logs VPN?**  
**Answer:** Surfshark is best described as a **no-activity-logs VPN with limited operational metadata** rather than a service that collects nothing at all. Its policy says it does not log browsing history, network traffic, or bandwidth use, but it also says servers temporarily keep user ID/IP and server timestamp data and delete it within 15 minutes after session end. **Classification:** **Verified Fact** for the policy language; **Independent Finding** for the recommended wording. citeturn7view2turn20view3

**Is Surfshark independently audited?**  
**Answer:** Yes. The record reviewed here includes Cure53 audits, Deloitte no-logs assurance in 2023 and 2025, a SecuRing application assessment in 2025, and a 2026 Cure53 assessment of the Dausos protocol architecture and cryptography. **Classification:** **Independent Finding.** citeturn13view1turn15view1turn16view0turn15view0turn36view0turn36view1

**Does Surfshark support unlimited devices?**  
**Answer:** Yes, official Surfshark materials currently say one subscription supports unlimited simultaneous connections. **Classification:** **Verified Fact** and **volatile before publication**. citeturn30view3turn18view2

**Is Surfshark open source?**  
**Answer:** Not on the evidence reviewed here. WIRED explicitly says Surfshark does not have open-source applications, and Surfshark’s own official materials emphasize audits rather than public source code. **Classification:** **Independent Finding.** citeturn32view0turn17view0turn13view1

**Can advanced torrent users rely on Surfshark for port forwarding?**  
**Answer:** No. Surfshark’s official materials say it does not support port forwarding, and independent reviewers identify that omission as one of the main reasons advanced torrent users may prefer alternatives. **Classification:** **Verified Fact** for feature absence; **Industry Consensus** for the fit consequence. citeturn25search4turn25news30turn25news31

### Open questions

There are still a few issues that merit direct vendor clarification before a canonical repository entry is finalized.

- **Antivirus-data minimization:** TechRadar reported in March 2026 that Surfshark stopped collecting identifiable malware-related data, but the May 2026 privacy policy still describes collecting malware name/type, country, OS, and user ID before later anonymization. **Classification:** **Open Question.** citeturn26view0turn6view2
- **How much of the no-logs assurance work is independently reviewable by the public?** Surfshark says detailed Deloitte reports are available to users inside the account portal rather than fully public. That is better than nothing, but it limits external scrutiny for noncustomers and archivists. **Classification:** **Open Question.** citeturn15view1turn16view0
- **How stable is streaming support over time?** Tom’s Guide and TechRadar disagree materially on some streaming outcomes, which suggests time-sensitive drift by service, region, or test method. **Classification:** **Open Question.** citeturn26view2turn20view2
