## Core Findings

### Free storage

Google currently includes 15 GB with a Google Account, shared across Drive, Gmail, and Google Photos. Shared folders generally consume the original owner's quota, not the recipient's, unless the recipient creates copies or adds owned files.

Dropbox Basic currently begins at 2 GB. That is simpler to understand but fills quickly with photos or video. Shared-folder behavior and over-quota restrictions can affect the practical allowance.

Decision: Google Drive offers the stronger free starting point, but the 15 GB must never be described as Drive-only.

### Sync and offline access

Google Drive for desktop supports Windows and macOS and can stream or mirror files. Streamed offline files can be removed when an account disconnects. Current official installation documentation does not provide a Linux desktop app.

Dropbox documents Windows, macOS, and Linux desktop support. It supports online-only files, offline files, and selective sync. Deleting an online-only file deletes it across Dropbox, which reinforces that online-only storage is still synchronized storage.

Decision: Dropbox earns the stronger focused sync and Linux fit. Google Drive remains strong on Windows and macOS, especially for Google-native files.

### Collaboration

Google Drive's clearest advantage is Docs, Sheets, Slides, comments, and real-time Google-account collaboration. Google-native files open in the browser and can be exported to conventional formats.

Dropbox supports file collaboration, comments, Paper, and third-party integrations, but it is better treated as a file layer than a replacement for Google Docs collaboration.

Decision: Google Drive is the stronger collaboration default when Google-native documents matter.

### Sharing, ownership, and offboarding

In consumer My Drive, files remain individually owned. Folder permissions can be inherited, and editors may be able to move or delete files depending on the role. Transferring a folder does not automatically transfer every contained file.

Google Shared Drives use team ownership and retain files when members leave, but Shared Drives are work/school features rather than a standard consumer My Drive capability.

Dropbox personal folders and shared folders also depend on the owner and membership rules. Team ownership and administration are separate from a personal Basic or Plus account.

Decision: neither product has a universal ownership advantage. Buyers must compare the exact personal, family, work, or team plan.

### Recovery and versions

Google Drive Trash currently retains items for 30 days unless permanently deleted. Item ownership affects what happens when a non-owner removes a file.

Dropbox currently documents 30 days of version history for Basic, Plus, and Family, longer windows for several higher plans, and no restoration of permanently deleted items. Longer history starts when the plan changes and is not retroactive.

Decision: Dropbox provides clearer plan-level version-history documentation; Google provides a clear consumer Trash window. Neither is a complete backup.

### File-size limits

Google Shared Drive documentation permits files up to 5 TB within account, storage, and daily upload constraints. Dropbox documents a 2 TB maximum file, recommends desktop/API paths for very large files, and warns browser uploads above 375 GB may be unreliable.

Decision: Google has the higher documented ceiling, but both exceed the needs of most beginners. Storage quota and upload method are usually more relevant.

### Privacy

Google documents processing Drive content for service and security functions and states that Drive content is not used for advertising. Standard consumer Drive is not ordinary end-to-end encrypted file storage.

Dropbox's standard service is also not positioned as consumer zero-knowledge storage. Security documentation describes infrastructure and account controls, but provider-managed storage remains distinct from an E2EE architecture.

Decision: neither wins for users whose primary requirement is provider-inaccessible file contents. Those users should compare the broader field in the beginner cloud-storage guide.

### Paid value

Google One offers smaller storage increments and can share storage with family members where available. It also expands the same pool used by Gmail and Photos.

Dropbox's personal upgrade is a much larger move from the 2 GB free tier and may fit users who need substantial conventional-file storage and focused sync.

Decision: Google is usually the better paid fit for modest extra capacity; Dropbox makes more sense when its sync experience and larger allocation are both useful. No fixed promotional price is required for this conclusion.

### Migration and portability

Google Drive can export Docs, Sheets, Slides, files, comments, and selected metadata, but conversion can change formatting, links, suggestions, scripts, or workflows. Shared ownership and permissions may not transfer.

Dropbox primarily stores conventional files, which generally reduces format lock-in, but versions, comments, links, sharing state, and ownership still do not migrate automatically.

Decision: Dropbox has the portability advantage for conventional files. Every buyer should test an export before committing a large library.

## Reader Scenarios

- Choose Google Drive for Gmail/Photos/Docs households, students using Google accounts, and live Google-native collaboration.
- Choose Dropbox for a neutral sync folder, Linux desktop use, conventional file portability, and mixed ecosystems.
- Use both only with clearly separated purposes; overlapping sync roots create confusion and duplicate storage.
- Choose neither when end-to-end encryption is the primary requirement; compare Proton Drive, Sync.com, pCloud Crypto, or another researched option.
- Keep an independent backup regardless of the choice.

## Conflicting or Ambiguous Evidence

- Google storage is widely summarized as 15 GB free, but that amount is shared with Gmail, Photos, Android backups, and other counted data.
- Dropbox's free allowance may receive referral bonuses, but the stable documented starting point remains 2 GB.
- “Backup” appears in both providers' product language, but synchronized folders, camera uploads, computer-folder backup, version history, and independent backup are not interchangeable.
- Recovery differs by item ownership, plan, retention window, and permanent-deletion state.
- Shared Drives and Dropbox team spaces should not be compared as if they were included with ordinary consumer accounts.
- Annual-equivalent prices may require prepayment and renew automatically; regional pricing and taxes vary.

## Editorial Decision

Core verdict:

- Google Drive is better for most beginners who value a larger free starting point, live collaboration, and integration with Gmail, Photos, Android, and Google apps.
- Dropbox is better for focused conventional-file sync, Linux desktop support, platform neutrality, and easier format portability.
- Neither is the default choice for end-to-end encrypted storage.
- Neither replaces an independent backup.

The article should lead with this fit-based decision, explain shared quota and ownership prominently, and avoid price, speed, reliability, or recovery claims that the evidence does not support.
