## Purpose

Create a skeptical travel VPN guide that helps readers choose based on trip context, destination risk, devices, public Wi-Fi use, streaming expectations, privacy needs, support, and short-term versus long-term use.

This is a product-selection article, not a universal "you need a VPN to travel" article.

## Distinct Search Intent

The primary intent is: Which VPN should I choose for travel?

This is distinct from:

- Are VPNs Worth It? - whether a VPN solves a real problem.
- Best VPNs for Beginners - general beginner VPN recommendations.
- Best Free VPNs - no-cost VPN tiers.
- Free VPN vs Paid VPN - whether to pay.
- VPN Buying Guide - general evaluation criteria.
- Product reviews - individual provider evaluation.
- Comparison pages - direct two-product choices.

## Source Conflicts and Uncertainty

- Proton VPN country/server counts differ across current official support pages. The article should state that paid-plan coverage must be rechecked immediately before publication or purchase.
- ExpressVPN device limits have conflicting official wording: one page says up to 10 or 14 simultaneous connections, while a router-focused page still references 8. The article should say limits depend on subscription and must be verified at checkout.
- ExpressVPN refund terms are also nuanced: the guarantee page presents a 30-day first-time-user refund, while pricing/terms snippets show exclusions for some promotional or renewal contexts.
- Mullvad's server page is live/status based, so exact country counts should not be frozen into evergreen copy unless rechecked.
- Streaming access is inherently volatile and should be described as inconsistent, never guaranteed.
- VPN legality varies by destination and can change. The article should use durable guidance instead of a country-by-country legality chart.
- No hands-on travel testing, speed testing, leak testing, streaming testing, hotel Wi-Fi testing, airport Wi-Fi testing, or restrictive-country testing was performed for this article.

## Legal and Destination Guidance

- Use high-level legal guidance only.
- Link to State Department travel advisories for destination-specific risk context.
- Explain that laws and network policies vary by country, school, workplace, hotel, airline, and service.
- Do not provide evasion playbooks for government restrictions.
- Tell readers to install and test apps before departure, keep recovery codes, and follow local law.

## Safety Guidance

- FTC public Wi-Fi guidance says modern encryption makes many public Wi-Fi sessions safer than old scare stories, but users should still verify HTTPS and avoid risky behavior.
- EFF guidance emphasizes threat modeling and the trust shift from ISP/local network to VPN provider.
- A VPN does not prevent phishing, malware, scams, weak passwords, compromised accounts, or unsafe downloads.

## Evidence Gaps

- No provider was tested on hotel, airport, cruise, campus, or airline Wi-Fi.
