## Core Verdict

Many students do not need a paid VPN. Start with a reputable free option if the goal is light public-Wi-Fi use or basic privacy on a tight budget. Pay when free limits become real friction: multiple devices, regular streaming attempts, study abroad, a semester-long need, better support, or consistent reliability. Campus policy, managed devices, exam software, and local law matter more than convenience.

## Campus, Dorm, Public Wi-Fi, and Policy Evidence

- FTC public Wi-Fi guidance says widespread HTTPS makes public Wi-Fi usually safer than older advice suggested, but users still need encryption checks, account hygiene, scam awareness, and safer behavior.
- EFF VPN guidance emphasizes threat modeling and the trust shift: a VPN can hide traffic from a local network or ISP, but it shifts trust to the VPN provider and does not solve every privacy problem.
- University acceptable-use and VPN policies show that schools can set network rules, approved VPN use, authentication, and resource restrictions. Students should follow institutional policies and should not use a consumer VPN to bypass school controls.
- Managed school devices, school-issued laptops, and exam/proctoring systems can be governed by institutional software and policy; a VPN should not be framed as a way around monitoring or academic rules.
- Captive portals and blocked VPN traffic can interrupt VPN use on campus, dorm, hotel, and travel networks.
