## Research objective and distinct intent

Help mainstream Windows users decide whether to keep Microsoft Defender or pay for Bitdefender, Norton, ESET, F-Secure, McAfee, TotalAV, or another product. This is a current evidence-and-subscription decision guide, distinct from the planned educational page Is Antivirus Still Necessary?, the future Windows Defender vs Paid Antivirus comparison, and the dedicated TotalAV Review.

## Scope and candidate set

Primary emphasis is Windows 11 because the strongest comparable current lab set is Windows consumer testing. Mac, Android, and iOS coverage is treated as plan/platform context, not proof that every app supplies identical antivirus capability.

Candidates evaluated: Microsoft Defender, Bitdefender, Norton, ESET, F-Secure, McAfee, Avast, AVG, Avira, Malwarebytes, TotalAV, Trend Micro, Sophos Home, Kaspersky, G Data, K7, Panda, Total Defense, VIPRE, Quick Heal, and Webroot.

## Evaluation criteria

1. Current multi-lab protection evidence.
2. False-positive evidence.
3. Independently measured system impact.
4. Test continuity rather than one-month peaks.
5. Platform and device coverage.
6. Core protection versus optional bundle features.
7. First-term and renewal transparency.
8. Cancellation and refund clarity.
9. Ownership, privacy, enforcement, and geographic constraints.
10. Reader fit and limitations.

## Candidate analysis and inclusion decisions

### Microsoft Defender — best built-in baseline for many Windows users

Defender is included with Windows 11 and had 18/18 in AV-TEST April 2026, a long run of strong AV-TEST scores, and a 100% protection/legitimacy result in SE Labs Q4 2025. AV-Comparatives Jul–Oct 2025 recorded 99.1% protection. It is the default recommendation for ordinary Windows users who do not need a paid suite, not a universal winner.

### Bitdefender — leading balanced paid choice

Bitdefender had 18/18 in AV-TEST April 2026 and was a 2025 AV-Comparatives Top-Rated product. Its annual record included strong malware protection and low false positives, even though it did not lead every real-world test. The provider clearly distinguishes introductory and renewal pricing, but actual checkout terms remain volatile.

### Norton — leading full-suite fit

Norton had 18/18 in AV-TEST, 100% protection in AV-Comparatives Jul–Oct 2025, and low measured impact in the 2025 summary. Its published U.S. pages disclose first-year and renewal examples. It is a fit-based suite recommendation, not proof every bundled extra is best in class.

### ESET — strong focused paid alternative

ESET had 17.5/18 in AV-TEST April 2026, 99.5% in AV-Comparatives Jul–Oct 2025, and a 2025 Top-Rated designation. The page may describe it as a strong alternative but may not call it light, quiet, or best for advanced users without CBT testing.

### F-Secure — cross-platform alternative with current Mac evidence

F-Secure had 17.5/18 in the Windows April 2026 test and 18/18 in AV-TEST's March 2026 macOS test. It remains an alternative because cross-platform plan value and workflow were not tested by CBT.

### McAfee — credible suite alternative

McAfee had 18/18 in AV-TEST, 99.5% in AV-Comparatives Jul–Oct 2025, and 100% SE Labs Q4 2025 protection/legitimacy. Bundle and renewal value require reader-specific verification.

### TotalAV — credible protection, not a leading recommendation

TotalAV had 18/18 in AV-TEST, 99.5% in AV-Comparatives Jul–Oct 2025, a 2025 Approved Product award, and high-90s SE Labs Q4 2025 results. It was not a 2025 AV-Comparatives Top-Rated product. Its direct affiliate program and affiliate-heavy promotion increase the need for restraint. Official terms describe recurring subscriptions, add-ons, and refund windows. It belongs in the table and dedicated review, not the top recommendation.

### Avast / AVG — strong free protection with a material privacy history

Both tested strongly. The FTC's finalized Avast order over Jumpshot browsing-data practices materially affects trust analysis and must be visible. Current scores do not erase dated conduct; dated conduct does not prove current identical behavior.

### Malwarebytes, Avira, Trend Micro, Sophos and others

These products remain alternatives or monitors based on uneven cross-lab continuity, weaker results in a relevant test, missing participation in a current set, or narrower fit. They are not omitted because of affiliate status.

### Kaspersky — excluded for U.S. readers

Lab performance remains strong, but the U.S. Commerce prohibition makes it unsuitable for CBT's U.S.-oriented recommendation set. The exclusion is geographic/legal, not a false claim that the engine performs poorly.

## Pricing and value treatment

Do not use a cheapest-product label. Show Defender as included with Windows, distinguish paid first-term and renewal pricing, and tell readers to compare device count and the core antivirus separately from VPN, identity, backup, cleanup, and password-manager bundles. Numeric prices should be limited to verified examples or omitted when dynamic.

## Hands-on testing status

No product was installed or purchased. CBT did not run malware, EICAR, benchmarks, scans, network capture, removal, cancellation, refunds, or support contacts. Interface and performance claims must be attributed to labs or omitted.

## Provisional verdict

- Keep Microsoft Defender when built-in protection covers the reader's needs.
- Choose Bitdefender for the strongest balanced paid evidence in this review.
- Choose Norton when a broad suite is worth the renewal cost.
- Consider ESET, F-Secure, or McAfee for fit-specific alternatives.
- Treat TotalAV as capable protection with meaningful subscription and trust caveats, not the top pick.

## Prohibited claims

No product is “unhackable,” “100% safe,” guaranteed to remove every threat, permanently fastest, universally lightest, or universally best. Do not claim CBT tested products. Do not call TotalAV a scam. Do not call Kaspersky ineffective.

## Refresh triggers

New lab cycles; product/version changes; plan, renewal, device, refund, or cancellation changes; ownership or enforcement action; incident or flawed update; affiliate implementation; or hands-on testing.
