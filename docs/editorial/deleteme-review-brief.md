# Editorial Objective

Create an evidence-based DeleteMe review for mainstream privacy buyers.

The article must help readers understand whether DeleteMe is a good fit for their needs without exaggeration, unsupported rankings, hype, or affiliate influence.

The review should position DeleteMe as a legitimate, established, human-assisted data-removal service. It should frame DeleteMe as strong for mainstream U.S. consumers who want a hands-off people-search/data-broker removal service, users who value human assistance, public-facing professionals who want custom removals and support, businesses/admin buyers, and families or multi-person users where supported. It should clearly explain why DeleteMe is weaker for users who want the best independently measured removal performance, low-cost automation, screenshot-heavy proof, DIY control, or full internet deletion.

# Editorial Guardrails

Do not say DeleteMe deletes users from the internet.

Do not imply removals are permanent.

Do not imply DeleteMe removes everything from Google.

Do not imply DeleteMe removes court records, government records, public records, social media posts, blogs, forums, dark-web leaks, or all copied / republished data.

Do not treat the full 976-site claim as equivalent to Standard consumer-plan coverage.

Do not ignore that Standard consumer coverage appears materially narrower than the broad master-site-list headline.

Do not ignore Consumer Reports' finding that DeleteMe was a midlevel performer in its people-search removal test.

Do not ignore Consumer Reports' finding that manual opt-outs outperformed all tested paid services in that narrow test.

Do not overstate DeleteMe's effectiveness.

Do not ignore academic and regulatory context showing category-wide limits.

Do not ignore the privacy tradeoff of giving DeleteMe sensitive personal identifiers.

Do not treat SOC 2 or ISO claims as public proof of every security or outcome detail.

Do not hard-code pricing unless clearly marked as volatile and sourced from the approved research.

Do not hard-code affiliate links.

Do not let affiliate availability or commercial status influence editorial conclusions.

Do not invent testing results.

Do not create unsupported rankings.

Preserve uncertainty where the research identifies unresolved questions.

# Required Nuance

The review should clearly explain:

- DeleteMe is a credible, established, human-assisted data-removal service.
- DeleteMe is strongest for mainstream U.S. consumers who want a known provider and hands-off help.
- DeleteMe is strongest for people-search and data-broker exposure reduction, not full internet deletion.
- DeleteMe's strongest lane is human assistance, reporting, recurring cleanup, maturity, support, custom removals, and business/admin features.
- Data removal is ongoing maintenance, not a one-time permanent deletion.
- Data can reappear.
- The full 976-site claim includes plan markers, higher-tier targets, international targets, and custom-request categories; it must not be treated as Standard consumer coverage.
- Consumer Reports found DeleteMe was a midlevel performer and found manual opt-outs outperformed all tested paid services in a narrow people-search test.
- That does not make DeleteMe fake; it means expectations should be realistic.
- DeleteMe requires sensitive personal identifiers to work.
- DeleteMe has stronger-than-average trust/security signals, but public audit/assurance detail should not be overstated.
- Public-record, Google-result, social-media, and dark-web limitations must be visible.
- Incogni may be better for simpler automation and pricing clarity.
- Optery may be better for screenshot proof, exposure visibility, and free reports.
- Privacy Bee may appeal to users who want broader or more complex privacy workflows, but details need verification.
- Kanary may appeal to threat-model or doxxing/stalking-oriented users.
- Manual opt-out may be better for disciplined DIY users with time and control needs.
