# Device Security Cluster Implementation Plan

Status: Draft implementation plan  
Project: Choose Better Tech  
Date opened: 2026-07-19  
Publication status: Not authorized

## Purpose

Translate the approved next-cluster recommendation into a controlled research, testing, and publishing sequence for a beginner-focused consumer device security cluster.

This document authorizes planning and evidence collection only. It does not authorize article publication, rankings, affiliate activation, production changes, or live-malware testing.

## Strategic question

The cluster should begin with the real beginner decision:

> Do you still need antivirus on Windows 11?

The cluster must answer that question without fear-based marketing, false certainty, or treating every user as having the same risk profile.

## Initial cluster structure

### Stage 1 — trust and decision foundation

1. **Do You Still Need Antivirus on Windows 11?**
   - role: primary decision guide and likely cluster entry point;
   - intent: determine when built-in protection may be enough and when additional protection may be justified;
   - commercial posture: non-ranking, non-affiliate-first.

2. **How Antivirus Software Actually Works**
   - role: architecture explainer;
   - intent: signatures, behavioral detection, cloud reputation, web protection, ransomware controls, false positives, and update dependence;
   - boundary: no claim that one detection method guarantees protection.

3. **Microsoft Defender vs Third-Party Antivirus**
   - role: practical comparison framework;
   - intent: compare protection layers, usability, system impact, support, privacy, bundled extras, and cost;
   - boundary: avoid turning benchmark scores into a single definitive winner.

### Stage 2 — practical buyer guidance

4. **Best Antivirus for Beginners**
   - role: commercial ranking only after evidence protocol is complete;
   - intent: identify products that are understandable, low-friction, and appropriately protective for non-experts;
   - boundary: no ranking before first-hand setup and usability testing.

5. **Free Antivirus vs Paid Antivirus**
   - role: value decision guide;
   - intent: distinguish core protection from paid extras, support, identity features, VPN bundles, password managers, parental controls, and upsells;
   - boundary: avoid assuming paid automatically means safer.

6. **Antivirus vs Internet Security Suites**
   - role: bundle-decision guide;
   - intent: determine when broader suites add useful protection versus duplicating tools a reader already has;
   - boundary: disclose overlap with VPN and password-manager products already covered by CBT.

### Stage 3 — focused use cases

7. **Best Antivirus for Families**
8. **Best Lightweight Antivirus for Older PCs**
9. **Best Antivirus for Privacy-Conscious Users**
10. **Can Antivirus Slow Down Your Computer?**

These are provisional and must pass intent-distinctness and evidence-feasibility review before drafting.

## Evidence protocol

### Allowed first-hand testing

- install and uninstall experience;
- account creation and cancellation flow;
- onboarding clarity;
- default settings and permission prompts;
- scan controls and scheduling;
- notification frequency and upsell behavior;
- browser-extension behavior;
- visible privacy controls;
- system-resource observations during normal use;
- support accessibility;
- refund and cancellation workflow where safe and authorized;
- feature availability by plan.

### Prohibited testing

- downloading or executing live malware;
- disabling host protections to expose the test machine;
- using real phishing credentials or personal accounts in unsafe scenarios;
- testing ransomware against personal files;
- publishing exploit instructions;
- claiming laboratory-grade protection results from informal testing;
- reproducing copyrighted benchmark tables or proprietary report content.

### External evidence hierarchy

Use sources in this order:

1. official operating-system and security-product documentation;
2. independent testing laboratories with disclosed methods;
3. government or recognized security-agency guidance;
4. vendor security advisories, privacy policies, and plan documentation;
5. reputable technical research and incident reporting;
6. user reports only for recurring usability signals, never as sole proof of security effectiveness.

### Required claim categories

Every draft must separate:

- observed first-hand fact;
- independent laboratory result;
- provider claim;
- inference;
- recommendation;
- unresolved question.

## Product test shortlist

The initial shortlist should include built-in Microsoft Defender as the baseline plus a small cross-section of third-party products representing different business models and product designs.

Provisional candidates:

- Microsoft Defender;
- Bitdefender;
- ESET;
- Norton;
- Avast or AVG, treated carefully because of historical privacy and ownership considerations;
- Malwarebytes, with scope clearly distinguished from full-suite antivirus positioning.

This shortlist is not a ranking and does not imply endorsement. Final inclusion requires current availability, test feasibility, privacy review, and clear intent contribution.

## Scorecard dimensions

No single composite score should hide material tradeoffs. Record at minimum:

- protection evidence quality;
- false-positive evidence;
- system-impact evidence;
- setup clarity;
- default protection posture;
- notification and upsell pressure;
- privacy and data-use transparency;
- cancellation and refund friction;
- support accessibility;
- useful extras versus duplicated bundle features;
- price and renewal volatility;
- suitability for beginners.

## Required research package before drafting article 1

1. Windows 11 built-in security baseline.
2. Independent-lab methodology comparison.
3. Current third-party product shortlist and plan map.
4. Privacy-policy and telemetry review.
5. First-hand test environment plan.
6. Claim ledger for the lead article.
7. Cannibalization review against existing VPN, password-manager, data-removal, and cloud-storage content.
8. Internal-link plan.
9. Volatile-claim refresh schedule.
10. Owner approval of the first article charter.

## Test environment requirements

- use a dedicated or resettable Windows 11 test environment;
- do not use the owner's primary personal machine for risky experiments;
- retain no personal files in the test environment;
- document hardware, Windows version, update state, product version, plan, and test date;
- take sanitized screenshots only;
- record install/uninstall residue and default-setting changes;
- restore the environment between products where practical;
- never test two real-time antivirus products simultaneously unless the products explicitly support coexistence.

## First article charter — provisional

### Working title

Do You Still Need Antivirus on Windows 11?

### Reader decision

Help a non-expert decide whether built-in Windows protection is sufficient for their situation or whether third-party antivirus adds enough value to justify cost, complexity, privacy tradeoffs, and possible duplication.

### Required sections

- what Windows 11 already includes;
- what antivirus can and cannot do;
- which user behaviors and threat models change the answer;
- when Microsoft Defender may be enough;
- when third-party protection may add useful value;
- what paid suites often bundle;
- what to check before installing anything;
- decision matrix by user type;
- limitations and evidence dates.

### Prohibited framing

- “everyone needs paid antivirus”;
- “Defender is all anyone will ever need”;
- fear-based infection claims;
- absolute security guarantees;
- benchmark-score cherry-picking;
- affiliate availability as editorial justification.

## Sequence and gates

1. Approve this implementation plan.
2. Complete the research package.
3. Approve the test shortlist and safe test environment.
4. Conduct first-hand usability testing.
5. Produce the lead-article evidence dossier and charter.
6. Draft the lead article.
7. Run editorial, claims, cannibalization, and independent-review gates.
8. Publish only after explicit owner approval.
9. Use lead-article findings to decide whether the remaining provisional cluster still deserves production.

## Owner-only decisions

- approve product purchases or trials;
- approve the dedicated test environment;
- authorize cancellation/refund tests;
- approve the final product shortlist;
- approve each article charter;
- approve publication and affiliate activation separately.
